Launching on B2B
Section 04 · Launching on B2B

How the strongest B2B go-lives think about value

  • Reduce manual work — fewer phone, email, and spreadsheet orders; reps focus on exceptions
  • More self-serve ordering — buyers place orders online instead of calling or emailing in
  • Catalog online — searchable SKUs and assortments, not PDFs
  • D2C-like B2B UX — fast, on-brand storefront flows; not a "portal afterthought"
  • Order history & reorder — past orders act as templates for repeat buyers
  • Pay in Checkout — cards, vaulted methods, clear paid/unpaid state; fewer surprises

Key tools you build with

  • Companies & locations
  • B2B catalogs & price lists
  • Markets for international
  • Flow for automation
  • Sidekick & Magic for AI
  • Functions & UI extensions
Section 04 · Launching on B2B

Four launch moves — what moves the needle

🔌 Integrations-first

#1 technical risk: slips and churn. Model early, integrate, own cutover.

→ Involve us early on integration

🔥 Change management

Weak B2B or a big-bang? Plan onboarding — adoption + reorders takes time.

→ Phased rollouts, comms & training

👻 Avoid silent quitting

Payments pivot or scope drift mid-build? Loop us in — we advise or escalate.

→ Tell us before scope drifts

💳 Payments & order state

Orders paid outside Shopify often never get marked paid — bad UX and VPF doesn't trigger.

→ Sync paid status + VPF from day one

Section 05 · B2B Product Direction

Where we still need better solutions

1 — B2B subscriptions

Contract terms, net invoicing, and reorders don't map neatly to consumer-style selling plans. Apps and ops models vary in maturity.

2 — Multi-destination in one checkout

Ship parts of one order to different company locations in a single B2B checkout. Native checkout currently assumes one ship-to per order.

Still worth customisation, but no widely proven out-of-the-box story today — expect trade-offs, workarounds, or ERP-led flows. Position with explicit risks, not as "Shopify can't."

Section 05 · B2B Product Direction

B2B as a network

When orders, buyer data, and relationships stay on Shopify, the hub merchant and downstream buyer orgs can compound — beyond email and spreadsheets alone.

1 net-new B2B merchant brings their entire value chain — suppliers, buyers, subsidiaries — potentially onto the platform.

The compounding logic

  • Hub merchant — your B2B client acts as the anchor
  • Buyer orgs — distributors, branches, subsidiaries; each a node
  • Data on-platform — orders, reorders, and relationships compound vs. email
  • Collective — dropship via another merchant's store; extend inventory
  • Investment signal — preorders and seasonal allocation workflows top-of-mind from Q3
